### What is a RFC?

A **Request For Comments** is a formal document usually published by the Internet Engineering Task Force(IEFT), that details
technical rules, specifications, protocols for the internet. Desipte the name of request a published RFC is a finalised standarded
**locked in place** rather than a draft.

They are published to create a permanent, open record of how different network protocols, like email, Wi-Fi, 
or web browsing should function. Without RFCs, a smartphone
in London might not be able to communicate with a server in Tokyo because their software wouldn't be speaking the same technical language.
This **interoperability** of different devices is one of many benefits of using RFCs.

### Why are they so important

While interoperability is huge, having a standardized way of doing things comes with other critical advantages:

* **A Single Source of Truth:** This leaves zero room for ambiguity, interpretation, or guesswork. If engineers all adhere to the rules, everything runs like clockwork. When developers disagree, the RFC settles the debate.
* **Preventing Corporate Monopolies:** All rules are open to the public, meaning everyone from solo developers to massive corporations must follow them. Protocols like HTTP, TCP, and UDP are the backbone of the internet, and no single entity can own them.
* **A Historical Blueprint:** RFCs document the evolution of the internet. They show not just *how* a protocol works, but *why* it was designed that way in the first place, capturing the reasoning of the original engineers.
* **Better end products**, By agreeing on the underlying "plumbing" companies don't have to reinvent the wheel. Instead, they can focus their resources on competing at the user level by building better interfaces, faster devices, or stronger security.


Personally, I think this historical blueprint is the coolest part. Once an RFC is made, it is locked in place for the rest of time. It is a peephole into an era before the internet boom. 

Reading **RFC 1855** (Netiquette Guidelines) feels *so* unapologetically 1995. You can see exactly how much the digital world has changed since then. The authors literally had to write official, standardised rules warning people not to send "flames" (angry messages), Asking to stop the use of local acronyms before the internt standardised the use of acronyms like "AFK" and "FYI", and explicitly defining how to type a `:-)` face. It captures a fleeting moment where people were just figuring out how to exist the internet, right before it exploded into the dominant medium of human existence.

Ultimately, this highlights the true importance of RFCs. In 1995, the general public was entirely unfamiliar with how to behave online. To scale the network without it collapsing into chaos, there had to be **ONE** centralised document to engrain these behavioral protocols into the culture. It proves a fundamental engineering truth: scaling the software is the easy part and scaling the humans using it is the real bottleneck.

## The Journey of an RFC

The journey of an RFC begins long before it gets an official number. The process is designed to be open and collaborative, meaning **anyone** can propose a new standard.

Here are the key stages a proposal goes through:

* **Internet-Draft (I-D):** A proposal starts as a temporary working document. Anyone can write an I-D and submit it to the IETF.
* **Working Group Review:** If an I-D gains traction, an IETF working group adopts it. These groups debate, test, and refine the technical details. 
* **Last Call:** Once the working group is satisfied, the draft enters a final review period. The broader internet community gets a chance to read the proposal and raise any last-minute security or technical issues.
* **Publication:** If the draft passes the final checks, it is approved, assigned a unique RFC number, and published by the RFC Editor. At this point, the document is **locked into history and can never be edited**.

## How RFCs are Phased Out

One question I always had was how are new standards made and old ones phased out. 

When a technical standard becomes outdated, it is never deleted or altered. Instead, the IETF uses a precise marking system to change its official status.

Every RFC has a metadata header, and when a new, better standard is approved, the old RFC is marked with one of two statuses:

* **Obsoleted by RFC [New Number]:** This means the old RFC is completely replaced by a newer version. The old document remains in the archive for historical purposes, but a big warning label tells engineers to use the new one instead. For example, the original HTTP/1.1 specification (RFC 2616) was obsoleted by newer, split-up specifications.
* **Updated by RFC [New Number]:** This means the original RFC is still mostly valid, but a smaller, newer RFC has been published to modify, fix a bug, or add a specific feature to it.

Additionally, if a standard is deemed broken, insecure, or no longer useful, its category can be officially moved to **Historic** status, signalling to the world that it should no longer be implemented in modern software.

### Key takeaways

RFCs provide one standard all developers can come to and follow, if done correctly, everything runs like clockwork. I've realised the same applies to writing good documentation. 

Good documentation is not just a rough collection of tips, code with what it does. is the RFC of a project. It must act as a single, inflexible source of truth that eliminates ambiguity and guesswork. Just as internet protocols ensure that a server in Tokyo can communicate with a phone in London, good documentation ensures that a developer reading the codebase today can perfectly understand the intent of the developer who wrote it years ago. It standardises the human element of software engineering, preventing chaos and ensuring everyone builds upon the exact same foundation.
